Godavari Datta

Godavari Dutta was born in pre-independence India. She inherited the traditional Madhubani art from her mother and played an important role in improving and spreading this art form. In her career of around 35 years, she taught more than 50,000 students and artists across the country. She gained national attention when, at the age of 93, she was honored with the Padma Shri award by the Government of India for her contribution to Madhubani art. Continuing her mother’s legacy, she took part in many art exhibitions in India and even visited Germany and Japan several times to showcase her Madhubani paintings. Gradually, her work became very popular and received global appreciation. In the 1980s, she started her own training institute to teach and guide new artists. Being closely connected to this art for so many years, she witnessed many changes. Natural colors have now been replaced by acrylic paints, and unlike earlier times when every painting was unique, now many copies of the same art are being made. Her journey and dedication make her a true inspiration in the world of Madhubani art.

Sita Devi

Sita Devi was a well-known artist of Madhubani art. Through her hard work and dedication, she brought this traditional art form out of the walls of homes and introduced it to people across India and around the world. She was born in 1914 and passed away in 2005. She made the Bharni style of Madhubani painting very popular. In recognition of her contribution, the Government of India awarded her the Padma Shri in 1981. Sita Devi was not only a great artist but also played an important role in the development of her village, Jitwarpur. Because of her efforts, the village got a primary school, then a secondary school, proper roads, and electric poles. She also started a project to teach Madhubani painting to more than 1,000 villagers to help them become socially and financially independent.

Malavika Raj

Malvika Raj was born in 1990 in the Samastipur district of Bihar. She is a Dalit Madhubani artist who brings a new perspective to traditional art. She studied at NIIFT, Mohali, and draws inspiration from the Kohbar style. Her paintings reflect the lives of Buddha, Dr. Ambedkar, and other social icons. Her work has been displayed on international platforms like the University of Edinburgh. Through Madhubani art, she tells stories of caste injustice and Dalit struggles. Though she has faced criticism, she has stayed true to her path. Malvika continues to use art as a tool for social change and awareness.

Mahalaxmi

Pushpa Kumari was born in 1969. She was raised under the guidance of her grandmother, Mahasundari Devi, a renowned Madhubani artist. Her paintings focus on social issues like HIV, female foeticide, and mythological themes, but from a spiritual perspective. Her art stands out for its emotional depth, fine detailing, and dramatic expressions, which make her unique among other artists. Pushpa not only reflects the world around her but also expresses her understanding of these issues through traditional Madhubani art in a thoughtful and creative way.
